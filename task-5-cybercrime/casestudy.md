## Task 5 - Cybercrime Case Study

### Case Study: Phishing Attack on a Financial Services Firm

In 2023, a mid-sized financial services firm experienced a targeted phishing attack that exposed critical vulnerabilities in its cybersecurity practices. The attack began with employees receiving emails that appeared to originate from the company’s IT department. The message urged recipients to reset their passwords due to a “security upgrade,” providing a link to a fraudulent website that closely mimicked the company’s official login portal.

Several employees, assuming the email was legitimate, entered their credentials. The attackers quickly harvested this information and gained unauthorized access to internal systems, including sensitive client financial data. Within hours, the compromised accounts were used to initiate unauthorized transactions and extract confidential records.

The attack went undetected for nearly 48 hours, primarily due to the absence of real-time monitoring systems and inadequate employee awareness. The breach was eventually identified when unusual transaction patterns triggered alerts in the banking system. By then, significant financial and reputational damage had already occurred.

A post-incident investigation revealed key issues: lack of multi-factor authentication (MFA), insufficient employee training on phishing detection, and weak email filtering mechanisms. The phishing email had subtle inconsistencies, such as a slightly altered sender domain and minor grammatical errors, which went unnoticed by employees.

In response, the company implemented stricter cybersecurity measures, including mandatory MFA, advanced email filtering systems, and regular employee awareness programs. Simulated phishing exercises were also introduced to improve vigilance among staff.

This case highlights how phishing attacks exploit human error rather than technical weaknesses. It underscores the importance of combining technical defenses with user education. Even well-structured organizations can fall victim if employees are not trained to recognize social engineering tactics. Ultimately, proactive prevention and rapid detection are essential to minimizing the impact of such attacks.
